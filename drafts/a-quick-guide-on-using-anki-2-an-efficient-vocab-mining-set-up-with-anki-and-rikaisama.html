So you want to:

  • read a Japanese novel and don’t end up crying yourself to sleep realizing you know nothing, Jon Snow?
  • learn Japanese by watching anime, but like, for real?
  • create beautiful Anki sets filled with handpicked vocabulary AND crystal-clear audio without spending days of mindnumbing copypasting?

Welcome to the lovely world of Integrated Anki & Rikaisama!

We’ve already established Rikaichan/kun as an incredibly helpful tool for reading Japanese texts online, yet one thing seemed missing still: a seemless integration with Anki for efficient vocabulary mining. A recent extension of the original Rikaichan, aptly named Rikaisama (it’s a pun!), adds several new features including communication with the Anki API and J-Pod101’s1 audiofiles. Concretely, this means audio playback of tens of thousands of words and expressions as well as easy integration of new vocab into Anki. This blog serves as a brief tutorial on setting up this environment as well as demonstrating some useful appliances.

Posibilities

Internet

This goes without saying. If you’re a relatively new learner, NHK provides simplified news articles and videos at NHK Easy News. I used these a lot near the end of my first year studying, and it’s a neat way to stay up to date as well.

Jnovel

「言い方がまちがってるわ。ウィン、ガー・デイアム　レヴィ・オーサ。『ガー』と長一くきれいに言わなくちゃ」2

I’ve used this method while reading The Girl Who Leapt Through Time during our book-reading sessions and just last week, as a kind of summer project, worked my way through a digital copy of the first Harry Potter this way (quite a high difficulty gap but I know the original by heart so that helped heaps context-wise).

Anime

A really cool concept recently released is Animelon’s multi-layer subtitled anime streaming web-app, akin to the Erin’s Challenge video’s (except with arguably way more interesting content: as of date there are over 60 series hosted, mostly all big names ranging from more recent hits as SAO and Attack on Titan to classics as Fullmetal Alchemist: Brotherhood and Clannad).

If I were to recommend one to start with, I’d definitely pick Clannad.3 The high-school setting means relatively easy and casual vocabulary (as opposed to, for example, SAO’s highly technical and fantasy-related vocab you’ll barely encounter in real life), while the voice acting’s pacing is calm enough to pick up as relative beginner, and devoid of difficult accents.

Check out their video demonstration below to see the various possibilities.

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XYR3_Yb9gBo

What bothers me a bit is the legality of it all. They’re currently keeping this site up as proof-of-concept in search of potential buyers but meanwhile they’re hosting full series in HD quality under de header of “educational purposes only”. I hope they survive, since this service is golden, really.

Gaming

This one’s a bit tricky still. The new web-possibilities since the implementation of the latest HTML-standard, HTML5, have given rise to a broad range of games running natively in your browser without the need of additional clutter such as Adobe Flash. Especially visual novels, a big thing in Japan, would be most suitable for our purpose, considering the large amounts of text. Except so far the online playable ones I’ve found were either already translated into English, or else of the early ’90s abandonware eroge kind, so not what I was looking for (you’ll easily find that online if that’s your cup of tea).

Another option is to set up your gaming environment in such a way it enables you to use these features. There’s an active community of technically capable Japanese learners at Reddit and Koohii who came up with such a thing. I’ve yet to try this myself, and honestly, it’s a bit bloated, but if you’re dying to play visual novels and learn Japanese at the same time, this’ll do the trick:

  1. Get Visual Novel Reader: it appears to be a mask to be run over an already installed visual novel, using OCR to capture Japanese text, parse it, and run it through some dictionaries (Tutorial).
  2. Use the Firgana Inserter Firefox Plug-in to automatically copy-paste dialogue from your clipboard to an empty Firefox screen.

If you’re running Firefox and your game side to side you’ll be able to max up your VN ankisets in no time, just like this guy!

https://youtu.be/7djqr-RG26s

Sources: https://www.reddit.com/r/visualnovels/wiki/vnhooking and http://forum.koohii.com/thread-10386.html

If there’s one downside to this method, it’s that audio will be added to your cards regardless if there’s actually an audio file in existence. More concretely, your audio-fields will have a reference to a non-existent file. Now, one solution is to, whenever you come across such a card during your anki-time, manually delete
